Creation Process

Matcha is taken from shade-grown tea plants which have high levels of chlorophyll, making the shade really deep green. As such, the buds with the top three layers are then harvested, their central core is removed, then they are grounded in stone mills. This meticulous process prevents the tea leaves from being oxidized (can often turn brownish or yellowish in color), and due to its high production standards, matcha is known to be one of the most expensive teas on the market (Britannica).

Matcha is a finely ground powder made from specially grown and processed green tea leaves, originating from Japan. The process of making matcha begins with selecting young tea leaves, which are shaded for about three weeks before harvest to boost their chlorophyll content and create a vibrant green color. After harvesting, the leaves are quickly steamed to halt oxidation, preserving their color and delicate flavor. The steamed leaves are then dried and the stems and veins are removed, leaving only the pure leaf, known as "tencha." The tencha is stone-ground into a fine powder, which is matcha. This grinding process is slow to avoid overheating and altering the subtle flavors of the tea. The final product is a bright green, fine powder that is whisked with hot water and consumed entirely, distinguishing it from other types of brewed teas where the leaves are discarded.
